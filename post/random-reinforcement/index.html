Random Reinforcement in Games

Oct 8, 2015   #luck  #psychology  #gamasutra 

Article: The Power of Random Reinforcement

Card-drawing: a Form of Random Reinforcement?

The author gives the example of card-drawing, such as that in games like MtG or Hearthstone, as a form of random reinforcement. While I’m familiar with the concept, I’ve always thought of it in the contexts of MMO loot drops and the gacha mechanics we so often see now in mobile games.

I suppose I don’t really consider card-drawing as a form of random reinforcemt because that feeling of hoping for a particular outcome isn’t very consistent throughout an entire match.

At the start of a match, you feel excited as you hope for a good starting hand. And in subsequent card draws, there may be specific scenarios in which you’re praying really hard for a specific card(e.g. a good 2-drop, a counter, combo, etc). But notice how the desired card changes from turn to turn, and also that in general, each card draw fills you with varying intensities of anticipation.

Not all card draws were created equal.

Perhaps for that reason, Hearthstone may be addictive as a game, but I don’t think anyone has ever been addicted to the specific act of drawing cards itself. (“Okay just one more card. Promise.”) Contrast this with the just-one-more-try sentiment when playing Blackjack or a slot machine.

Ethics vs Values

Is the use of random reinforcement in games ethical? I doubt that absolute morality exists, so I tend not to think in those terms these days. Arguments about ethics tend to accomplish very little anyway. Instead, I find it more productive to consider what I value personally, and let these values guide my decisions.

Personally, getting people addicted to gambling, to buying and opening card packs, or to grinding endlessly for loot isn’t very admirable. (Again, I’m not passing any judgement here; I’m merely staing that I don’t think I’d like to spend my time and effort on that.)

On the flip side, I think about a game like the Binding of Isaac which I’ve sunk countless hours into. A big part of its appeal comes from that anticipation before entering an item room or devil room, and knowing that I could get some super awesome item combo. Most roguelikes, with their random elements, tend to generate similar experiences. That’s random reinforcement right there.

For me, the Binding of Isaac is not just incredibly fun and addictive. It is also a beautiful game. So why is random reinforcement so distasteful in one scenario and yet palatable, even glorified perhaps in the other?

Is it a matter of degree? Of intention? Of context?

(Side note: The article mentioned that slot machines very frequently show an “almost win” oucome. Are the odds actually skewed towards that? That’s just crazy manipulative.)
