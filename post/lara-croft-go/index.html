Lara Croft GO

Nov 21, 2015   #puzzle  #game_notes  #controls 

I have a fondness for the Tomb Raider series.

When I was a kid, I played the first four games in the franchise as my dad sat beside me and gave suggestions when I inevitably got stuck. We bought guidebooks and somehow still managed to get lost sometimes. The first guidebooks were black and white, and the screenshots weren’t very clear. I remember how excited I was when they were finally printed in colour.

Lara Craft Go is the first Tomb Raider game I’d played since then and even though the gameplay was very different, it wafted nostalgia. It’s not just the radial menu and the occasional handstand. It’s that sense of wonder exploring ancient monuments filled with traps and treasure.

It’s well executed, and gorgeous.

Mechanics were gently introduced, and then reused and recombined. For example:
- Pressure plates could be deactivated by killing the creatures standing on them.
- Torches that protected you against the creatures could also “push” them back onto pressure plates.
- Pillars could stop rolling boulders, block dart traps, alter the creatures’ paths. They could be pushed onto pressure plates, onto creatures, and onto other pillars.

I think this is a concept Jon Blow and Marc Ten Bosch called “compatibility of mechanics” in their talk. (There are a few other “virtues” from the talk which applies to this game too.)

The controls for this game surprised me; I had expected the character to move to where I tapped, like in Monument Valley. Diagonal swipes felt a little unnatural at first, but were easy enough to get used to. This may be the first time I’ve seen a swipe-based control scheme in an isometric game.

I think something like the tracing controls in A Good Snowman Is Hard To Build would have worked well in this game. With turn-based games like this, it usually takes a few moves just to perform that one action you have in mind. In your head you’re thinking “I want to push that pillar there.”, but it translates into swipe, swipe, swipe to move to the pillar, and then swipe, swipe, swipe again to move it to your destination. It’s a very subtle thing, but I think the inefficient mapping from thought to execution makes the experience less smooth.

I surprised myself a little by enjoying this game, even if it wasn’t very challenging and many a time I was replaying levels and pixel-hunting for the secret vases in the game. (Aside: It amazes me how strange and nebulous and even hollow I still find the concept of fun – this subjective, swirling, conglomerate mess of even more abstract concepts like entertainment, enjoyment, engagement, amusement, challenge, meaning, etc.)

This is the first Tomb Raider game I’ve ever completed. No guidebooks too.
