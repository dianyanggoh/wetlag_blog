Flappy Bird's Difficulty

Jan 16, 2016   #difficulty  #one-button 

We tend to attribute the infuriatingly addictive nature of Flappy Bird to its maddening difficulty, but I think what really kept us playing was our constant indignation at how easy it should have been.

It’s sometimes easy to miss the difference when designing a game with a similar “frustration aesthetic”. Such a game should be way harder than it looks, and yet still feel fair.

(Maybe that’s why Swing Copters never took off(Pun!). That game looked hard.)

The difficulty ramp in Flappy Bird was a curious thing. Assuming that the probability of your hitting a pipe is 10%, the probability of getting past the 5th gap would be 1 - (1 - 10%)^5.

That’s approximately 40%, which is less than 10% * 5 = 50%.

If we associate probability with difficulty, this means getting past the fifth pipe isn’t five times as hard as passing the first; it’s more. The hundredth pipe feels so much more intense than the first because there is disproportionately so much more to lose. (Related: Michael Brough’s post on non-linear costs.)

Of course, we subconsciously recognise this without having to think through the math. Even as I write this I realize it’s kind of obvious, but I guess that’s part of why I keep this design notebook – to formalize and understand at a deeper level why some of these things are “obvious”.

Man, I miss that game.
